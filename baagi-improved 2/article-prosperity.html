Free Markets and the Great Enrichment

How economic freedom created unprecedented prosperity

Introduction

The last few centuries have witnessed a dramatic increase in living standards. This Great Enrichment refers to the explosion of prosperity that has lifted billions of people out of poverty and given today’s middle class luxuries unimaginable to past monarchs. At the heart of this transformation is economic freedom: when individuals are allowed to trade, innovate and cooperate without undue interference, they create wealth on a scale previously unseen.

Free markets create prosperity

The Cato Institute notes that liberty and free markets give us the incentives to use our talents and cooperate with others to produce and create. By unleashing these forces, free markets have lifted billions out of poverty and fueled what scholar Deirdre McCloskey calls the Great Enrichment【337816581703487†L195-L204】. In other words, the opportunity to buy and sell freely is not just an abstract ideal; it is the engine of material progress.

Despite these gains, the fight for economic freedom is not over. Governments continue to impose barriers that stifle growth, innovation and wealth creation【337816581703487†L195-L204】. Defending free enterprise means pushing back against protectionism, high taxes and regulations that discourage entrepreneurship.

Defending economic freedom

The case for capitalism is gaining unlikely allies. Cato observes that some of the world’s most influential voices are echoing its arguments【337816581703487†L205-L216】. On Joe Rogan’s podcast, entrepreneur Elon Musk quoted Milton Friedman while making the case for economic freedom; Senator Rand Paul called Johan Norberg’s The Capitalist Manifesto “a must‑read,” and venture capitalist Marc Andreessen dubbed Cato scholars the “patron saints of optimism”【337816581703487†L205-L216】. Such endorsements show that defending free markets isn’t just an academic exercise—it resonates with innovators and policymakers alike.

The lesson is clear: prosperity doesn’t come from government planning. It arises from people trading, experimenting and creating value for one another. To sustain the Great Enrichment, societies must continue to embrace competition, protect property rights and keep markets open.

Conclusion

Economic freedom has delivered miracles. Billions have escaped poverty, and the world is more innovative and interconnected than ever before. Yet those gains are fragile. Political pressures for protectionism and state control threaten to slow progress. Defending free markets—through sound policy, education and advocacy—is essential to ensure that the Great Enrichment continues for future generations.
